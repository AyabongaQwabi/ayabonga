LinkedIn will happily print **Senior** next to your name the moment a recruiter needs a keyword. Your family might clap when you get the certificate. The group chat might even send the GIF with the little crown.

None of that tells us whether you can **hold production** when the stand-up was vague, the spec was a voice note, and the only “staging environment” is courage.

## What the title sometimes hides

In bigger ecosystems, “senior” can mean years in the same stack, a certain salary band, or that you stopped asking permission before refactoring. In smaller SA teams — agencies, startups, civic projects — it often means **you are the one who gets the 9 p.m. message when something breaks.**

That is not glamour. It is responsibility.

## The township Wi‑Fi test (metaphorical but real)

I do not mean a literal speed test — though if you have never shipped a feature while tethering from a phone with 20% battery, you are missing a useful education.

I mean: **can you still think clearly when the conditions are not Silicon Valley?**

- When the client pays late but the deadline did not move  
- When “documentation” is a PDF from 2019 and a kind smile  
- When your junior is brilliant but exhausted, and someone has to model patience  

Senior is not only the code you write. It is the **temperature you set** in the room — even when the room is a Zoom and the load shedding app just turned red.

## What I try to practice

- **Explain the why**, not only the what, so the next person does not inherit a black box  
- **Leave breadcrumbs** — ADRs, READMEs, a decent handover — because memory is not a strategy  
- **Protect scope** without being cruel; push back with data, not ego  
- **Remember** that someone’s first “real” job might be on your team; your tone becomes their inner voice for years  

## The uncomfortable bit

You can be “senior” on paper and still learning. You can be mid-level and already carrying people well. Titles are **compression algorithms** — they lose nuance.

So take the promotion if it comes. Enjoy the raise. Then ask the harder question: **who is safer because you are in the building — or on the repo — today?**

That answer matters more than the label.
